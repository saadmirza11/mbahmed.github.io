Toothpaste Dispenser design challenge

The challenge was to design a fixture for a manufacturing line which could dispense toothpaste onto a toothbrush without using hands.
The purpose of this design challenge was to demonstrate skills in fixture design and manufacturing process development.

Requirements:
  1. Able to dispense toothpaste reliably with consistent volume
  2. Support manual or auto load and unload toothbrush process
  3. 22 seconds maximum cycle time
  4. Dispense within +/- 0.10 grams
  5. Toothpaste does not drip on the side
  6. Repeatable

Assumptions:
  1. All toothbrush are the same shape and size
  2. Toothbrush arrive to and leave the fixture on a conveyor
  3. Conveyor alignment is accurate within +/- 5mm longitudinal and +/- 1mm lateral, and is repeatable for 50 toothbrushes
  4. Toothbrush head is flat at the bottom
  5. Fixture is isolated from ground vibration caused by other factory equipment
Overview of fixture
1) Fixture
5 toothbrushes from the conveyor align with the fixture. The fixture table lifts so that the brushes rest on the workpiece holders. Alignment blocks on the workpiece holder are actuated by independent solenoids to align and restrict the brush in the X & Y axis. 
2) Dispense and Inspect
A CCD camera is used to check the alignment of the brush. If the brush is misaligned, the alignment blocks are retracted and closed to correct alignment, any subsequent misalignment triggers a line stop. Toothpaste is dispensed using a Hibar pump and Fisnar Valve and nozzle which were chosen to meet the +/- 0.10 gram dispense specification. While dispensing, the nozzle head is moved along the length of the brush at constant speed to evenly dispense toothpaste. The X&Y rail assembly, actuated by stepper motor and belt, moves the head. Upon completion, a UV light illuminates the toothpaste dispensed on the brush while the CCD camera captures an image to check if any toothpaste has dripped to the sides. Toothpaste contains fluoride which glows under UV light, hence an image captured under it will allow the toothpaste to be filtered easily from the surroundings.
3) Repeat and Transfer
The head moves to the next brush, and the dispense and inspect process is repeated. After all brushes are complete, the alignment blocks are retracted, the fixture table lowers to remove the brushes from the workpiece holder, and the conveyor moves forward.

Quadrotor Drone Battery Swapping Station
Final Year Design Project

Quadrotor drones cannot fly for more than 20 minutes due to their inability to carry heavy batteries with larger capacity. For commercial applications such as surveying, monitoring and security, where longer mission times are required, a human operator must be present to recharge or swap batteries of the drones. To overcome this problem, our solution was to develop a system that could swap depleted batteries of quadrotor drones autonomously with no operator assistance. We designed and built a proof of concept for a landing station that could achieve this using a custom designed battery enclosure, battery latching mechanism, landing pad built with alignment ramps, linear actuators for battery transfer, and a drone clamping mechanism. 
Contributions (within team of 4)
  • Designed the clamping mechanism and contributed to the design of the battery enclosure and latching mechanism in 3D CAD. Iterated through several designs and performed validation
  • Sourced parts and contributed to the fabrication and assembly using machine shop tools and equipment such as lathes and mills
  • Built the electronics hardware which consisted of an Arduino microcontroller, motor drivers, ultrasonic sensor, linear actuator and servo and stepper motors
  • Developed separate calibration and demo programs for the Arduino
The Magic Steps
  1. Drone lands on the platform, clamps engage automatically to secure the drone
  2. Discharged battery is removed from the drone and placed into the battery bay
  3. Fresh battery is removed from the battery bay and inserted into the drone
  4. Clamps disengage, the drone is ready to fly
Design
Clamp mechanism CAD model
Clamp mechanism prototype
A threaded rod mounted to a servo motor, that runs two clamps mounted in a T-formation. An internally-threaded block is mounted to the threaded rod. When the servo runs, the block moves forward. This causes two link arms to laterally push two clamps mounted to linear rails, outwards.
Battery enclosure and battery bay CAD model
Prototype. Battery enclosure with battery being inserted into bay on drone
Grab catch latch used to hold battery with enclosure inside bay
Servo motor actuated hook to grab battery
The battery is encased in a battery enclosure which can slide into and out of a battery bay on the Quadrotor drone. The battery bay consists of side rails with lead in chamfers and a grab catch latch. Both the bay and enclosure were machined on a mill. The grab catch consists of a strike plate which pushes into a spring loaded catch. It is rated at its closure force and was chosen to ensure it can hold the weight of the battery during flight. A hook actuated by a servo motor was attached to the end of a linear actuator which pulled and inserted the battery into the bay.

Torsion spring latch battery enclosure design
For the final prototype design, the battery storage was on a linear rack located beside the launch pad. In this design, the actuator is moved to the new position on linear rails using a belt and pulley drive system. However, in a previous design concept dubbed the "Mellennium Falcon", the batteries are stored in a circular array and the actuator is rotated to move the battery between the drone and the storage. This design was not chosen since it requires precise rotary control, a larger footprint, and limits the number of batteries in storage.
Another battery latch concept was to use a spring loaded snap fit type latch that would be pushed down to a disengage position by a solenoid on the actuator arm pulling/inserting the battery. This design was built in the machine shop, however significant binding occurred during unlatching due to the moment between the spring position, under the tab, and the actuation position. The design was modified to move spring position closer which reduced binding. However, the grab catch design proved to be more reliable.

Mass reduction of railway axle

Half model of railway wheel axle system
First iteration
Final design
The purpose of this project was to design a railway wheel axle system using the finite element software Abaqus. The objective was to minimize mass of the system while ensuring that it did not fail for a given set of loading conditions. Boundary conditions were determined by considering the application of the system. The rail axle, wheel and bearings were modeled as one object. Since the object and its loading/boundary conditions were symmetric along the x-z place, a half section was modeled with a symmetry boundary condition.
Increasing moment of inertia
Adding fillets and shoulders
Curved on both sides
The design was refined by incorporating solid mechanics concepts. For example, one of the loading conditions created a moment on the bottom half of one of the wheels. It was determined that the loading in this section was similar to that of a cantilever beam. Hence, the moment of inertia was increased while reducing the thickness of the section.

Several different contours were tested, and each design was further optimized using SolidWorks FEA Simulation. Using the design study feature, simulation was iterated with different dimensions of the features to find an optimized design with minimal mass. Once the design was refined in Solidworks Simulation, the CAD model was imported into Abaqus for detailed analysis using mesh optimization in the critical areas.
In order to ensure that simulation results are accurate, it is important to use adequate element type, geometric order and mesh density. Tetrahedral shaped elements were used with quadratic geometric order for the entire part. Mesh refinement was performed by reducing the node spacing in the high stress regions. Although a convergence study was also performed to verify mesh independence, a limit on the limit of elements in the education version of Abaqus did not allow the solution to converge.

Chevrolet Camaro Dynamics Modeling

Vehicle modelled in MSC Adams vehicle simulation software
Background
EcoCAR is an Advanced Vehicle Technology competition sponsored by U.S Department of Energy. The primary aim of this competition to encourage innovation in sustainable automotive technologies which would make the future cars more efficient and eco-friendly. For the EcoCar 3 competition, the University of Waterloo Alternative Fuels Team (UWAFT) is modifying a stock Camaro to a hybrid vehicle with two motors and a 850cc Weber engine. These change has led to addition of new components such as motors, mounts, battery systems, additional cooling systems, and removal or reduction in size of previously installed components such as engine, fuel tank as shown in the figure below. Hence, the weight distribution of the vehicle has shifted to the rear.

In order to achieve good dynamic stability, handling and comfort, the vehicle suspension elements including the spring, dampers and anti-roll bars may need to be replaced. Hence, the objective of this project was to develop a simulation environment where different suspension designs can be tested to maximize results in the competition. This project was completed within a group of 2.

Project results
A vehicle model and simulation environment similar to the skid pad test was developed in Adams. First order approximations were used for the inertial and suspension properties of the stock and 2016 Chevrolet Camaro.  Sample results from this simulation reveal that the stock vehicle has a tendency to understeer. Due to a lower and rearward center of gravity, the modified UWAFT Camaro exhibits very similar understeer behaviour.
​
Handling is defined as the response of a vehicle to driver input. Although it is a subjective evaluation, literature was found that proved a strong correlation between the subject feel of the handling to several measureable performance indicators such as roll angle and yaw rate. In the EcoCar 3 competition, the handling would be evaluated by the single lane change maneuver. Hence, a simulation environment was setup for a single lane change, as well as the double lane change maneuver. The results of these simulations found little difference between the stock and modified vehicles.

Vehicle Mass Estimation

This project was completed with a fellow coop student at SUTD. For the test vehicle, we used one of the autonomous golf carts being developed by the Urban Mobility group of the Singapore- MIT Alliance for Technology and Design (SMART) at the National University of Singapore. We fitted the golf cart with motor current and voltage sensors, IMU and a programmable ECU Controller. The ECU was programmed using a Simulink toolkit to detect acceleration events during a drive, measure and filter current and voltage signals and apply conservation of energy principles to estimate the vehicle mass. After optimization of the controller model parameters we achieved an accuracy of about 3%. I also designed a user interface in MATLAB to show the mass estimate results. CAN Bus Reverse Engineering

Vehicles have many ECUs that communicate data with each other over the CAN bus vehicle network. The on board diagnostic port on the car allows an external device to read data on the CAN bus. Universal standards which all vehicle must adhere to dictates how certain signals such as vehicle speed and error messages are reported on the CAN bus inorder to be read by the external scanner. However, many more signals such as door lock status and steering wheel angle are transmitted on the CAN bus. These signals do not adhere to any universal standards and are encoded differently across different models and manufacturers.

Using MATLAB, I developed a semi-automated tool that scanned the CAN bus for changes to messages in order to decode and effectively reverse engineer these non-standard OEM signals. I applied this tool to find the signals of the ABS speed sensor, steering angle sensor and door locks on a Mitsubishi i-MiEV.
